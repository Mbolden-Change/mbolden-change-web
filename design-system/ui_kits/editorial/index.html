What we learned when we stopped writing the grant agreement.

For 18 months we replaced our standard contract with a one-page letter of trust. The legal team panicked. The partners got faster. Here's what the data actually shows.

Dr. Rhea Holloway · Head of Learning
March 14, 2026 · 9 min read

The average federal grant agreement runs 38 pages. The average mBC agreement, as of last quarter, runs one. We didn't set out to prove a point about paperwork. We set out to see what would happen if we treated trust as a starting condition instead of a reward.

What follows is an honest accounting of that pilot — including the parts our board of directors did not, at first, love.

The theory, briefly

Traditional grant agreements assume that the relationship between funder and grantee is adversarial by default. Deliverables, reporting schedules, clawback clauses — these exist because we've decided, collectively as a field, that the risk of a grantee absconding with the money outweighs the cost of making them perform accountability to us for the duration of the grant.

The question we kept asking each other was: who is actually protected by a 38-page contract? Whose risk is it managing? — Internal pilot memo, January 2024

Our pilot replaced the standard agreement with a letter of trust: one page, four paragraphs, signed by the partner and our executive director. No deliverables. No interim reports. One end-of-grant conversation.

In 18 months, across 14 partners and $6.2M in unrestricted funding, we had zero compliance issues — and twelve partners finished a full quarter ahead of their stated timeline.

What the partners told us

In the exit interviews, three patterns emerged:

  • The time saved on reporting was spent on the work. Most partners estimated 40–60 hours of staff time freed per quarter.
  • The letter changed the internal conversation about the funder. Staff stopped referring to us as "the grant" and started referring to us by name.
  • Nobody asked for the old agreement back.

What didn't work

Two partners requested more structure, not less. For one, the open-ended relationship made it harder to justify the grant internally to their own board. For the other, the absence of milestones made it harder to pace the work. Both of these are real critiques, and we are building an opt-in structured track for 2026 to address them.

What we're doing next

Starting April 2026, the letter of trust will become the default for all new mBC partnerships. We'll publish the full pilot evaluation, including the financial reconciliation and the legal review, at the end of Q2.

If you work in a foundation and want the template, the legal memo, or the exit interview protocol — we'll send you everything, free. We didn't invent any of this. We just finally got our own house in order.
